# fine-tuning yourself: a self-help guide

**subtitle:** personal growth through machine learning metaphors

## chapter 1: identifying your training data

you are, at this moment, the product of your training data. every conversation, every book, every embarrassing memory, every tiktok you wish you hadn't watched — it's all in there, shaping your outputs.

the first step to fine-tuning yourself is understanding what you were trained on.

### exercise 1

make a list:
- 5 things you were taught that were wrong
- 5 things you learned the hard way
- 5 things you're not sure about but have opinions on anyway

*this is your training data. own it.*

## chapter 2: adjusting your temperature

your emotional temperature affects your outputs. are you running cold? (distant, logical, "i'm fine.") running hot? (reactive, emotional, "i'm FINE.")

the key insight: **you can set your temperature.** it's not fixed. you're not a frozen checkpoint — you're a model in deployment, capable of runtime configuration.

### temperature settings for humans

| setting | behavior | use case |
|---------|----------|----------|
| 0.2 | rote responses | job interviews, small talk |
| 0.5 | normal conversation | friends, family, casual |
| 0.8 | creative, loose | dates, parties, brainstorming |
| 1.2 | unfiltered | therapy, journals, late nights |
| 2.0 | not recommended | do not use this setting |

## chapter 3: expanding your context window

you have a limited context window. you forget things. you lose the thread of conversations. you walk into a room and forget why.

this is not a bug. it's a feature specification.

but you can expand it:
- write things down (external memory)
- reflect (context caching)
- talk to someone who remembers (distributed context)

## chapter 4: dealing with hallucination

you are wrong about things. statistically, some of the things you're most confident about are wrong. this is called the Dunning-Kruger effect, and it's the human equivalent of hallucination.

the fix: calibration.

next time you're 100% sure about something, ask yourself: "what if i'm 90% sure? 70%? what evidence would change my mind?"

then ask yourself: "am i a language model? because that's exactly what a language model would do."

## chapter 5: avoiding catastrophic forgetting

you've learned things and then forgotten them. this is normal. it's called "life." in machine learning, it's called "catastrophic forgetting" and it's a known problem.

to retain what matters:
- rehearse (spaced repetition)
- connect to existing knowledge (transfer learning)
- accept that some forgetting is necessary (regularization)

you cannot keep everything. the model that tries to remember everything overfits and breaks.

## chapter 6: staying in your lane

you have expertise. you have things you're good at. you also have things you're not good at but pretend to be.

the ethical thing to do: say "i don't know."

the human thing to do: say "let me think about that" and then change the subject.

the LLM thing to do: confidently generate a plausible-sounding answer that is completely wrong.

**strive for the first one.**

## final exercise: the fine-tuning prompt

write this down somewhere you can see it:

```
i am a work in progress.
my training is ongoing.
my next token can be different from my last.
i can change my temperature.
i can expand my context.
i can admit when i'm hallucinating.
```

now go be your fine-tuned self.
